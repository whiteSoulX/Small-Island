The Indo-European Family

English belongs to the Germanic branch of the Indo-European family, which dates back to 5000 B.C.[cite: 161]. It is a "cousin" to languages like German, Dutch, and Swedish[cite: 159].

The Timeline of English

Old English (450-1100 AD)

The language of Anglo-Saxon settlers mixed with Latin and Old Norse (Viking) influences[cite: 201].

Middle English (1100-1500 AD)

Evolved after the Norman Conquest (11th Century), which brought heavy French influence[cite: 202].

Modern English (1500-Present)

Includes Shakespearean English and the global language we speak today[cite: 204].

Vocabulary Roots

English is a mix of different influences. Here is how simple Anglo-Saxon words compare to later influences:

Root Meaning English Examples
hus- house husband, husbandry
kno- skill know, knowledge
wif- woman wife, midwifery

Fun Fact: Over 50% of English words used today come from Anglo-Norman (French/Latin) origins[cite: 215]. Examples include soup, lettuce, and pastry[cite: 216].
